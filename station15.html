OEBC Mock OSCE – Station 15

Oculomotor & Sensory Integrative • Colour Vision Deficiency (Interactive)

Step 1 – Read the Station

Scenario:
A 22-year-old male university student presents for a routine eye exam. VA is 20/20 OU. During testing, the patient misses several Ishihara colour plates and becomes visibly frustrated. He says: “No one has ever told me this. Does this mean I can't do certain jobs?” Ocular health is normal.

Your task: Explain the colour vision diagnosis, how it affects daily life, and discuss realistic occupational considerations. Provide reassurance and clear next steps while maintaining empathy.

Step 2 – Speak (Type) Your Response

Type what you would say to the patient:

Key Communication Points (What OEBC Expects)

  • Clearly state the diagnosis (likely congenital red-green deficiency).
  • Explain in simple terms how colour vision works and what causes the defect.
  • Reassure that this is common and stable throughout life.
  • Discuss realistic occupational considerations (e.g., aviation, police, electrician).
  • Emphasize that most careers remain fully accessible.
  • Validate the patient’s emotional response and provide supportive communication.
  • Offer options: further colour testing, counselling on adaptive strategies.

Self-Assessment Rubric

Score yourself 0–2 per category (0 = not addressed, 1 = partial, 2 = complete).

Domain Criteria
Diagnosis States congenital colour deficiency and what type is suspected.
Explanation Explains cone function and why certain hues are hard to differentiate.
Impact Addresses day-to-day challenges without exaggeration.
Occupational Guidance Mentions restricted fields AND reassures that most careers are unaffected.
Communication Empathetic, supportive, clear, and patient-centered.

Strong performance across these domains closely matches OEBC expectations for interactive stations.

AI-style Feedback (beta)

Example 10/10 response:
“Thanks for letting me know how you’re feeling about this. From your colour testing today, it looks like you have a congenital colour vision difference, most likely a red–green type. That means the cells in the back of your eye that detect certain colours work a bit differently, so some shades of red and green (and related colours) can be harder to tell apart.

This is actually quite common and it’s something you’re born with – it doesn’t usually get worse over time and it doesn’t lead to you going blind. Most people with this type of colour vision can do the vast majority of jobs and live completely independent lives. There are a few careers that have stricter colour requirements, like some roles in aviation, police work, or electrical trades, so if you’re interested in those we can look up the exact standards together and see where you stand.

Day to day, you’ve probably already found ways to adapt without realizing it – like using position instead of colour on charts or traffic lights, or asking for clarification when something is colour-coded. If you’d like, we can do more detailed colour testing and talk about practical strategies and resources. I’ll also note this in your chart so you don’t have to re-explain it every time you see an eye care provider. Do you have any questions or specific careers you’re worried about that we can talk through?”
