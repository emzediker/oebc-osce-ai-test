OEBC Mock OSCE – Station 22

Ocular Disorders • Bacterial Keratitis (Interactive)

Step 1 – Read the Station

Scenario:
A 27-year-old soft contact lens wearer presents with severe pain, redness, photophobia, and decreased vision in the right eye. They admit to sleeping in their lenses and occasionally rinsing them with tap water. Slit lamp (not shown) reveals a focal epithelial defect with a suppurative infiltrate and surrounding stromal edema.

Your task:
Explain to the patient what bacterial keratitis is, why it is serious, the role of contact lens hygiene, the need for urgent treatment, and the risks of delaying therapy. Provide clear, urgent but calm counselling.

Note: The real OEBC OSCE allows 8 minutes total for an interactive communication station.

Step 2 – Speak (Type) Your Response

Type what you would say to the patient:

Key Communication Points (What OEBC Expects)

  • Clearly state the diagnosis: bacterial keratitis (corneal infection).
  • Explain the seriousness: risk of corneal scarring, vision loss, or perforation.
  • Link the infection to their behavior (sleeping in CLs, tap water exposure) without shaming.
  • Stress that this is an ocular emergency requiring immediate intensive antibiotics.
  • Explain the treatment plan: strong drops, frequent dosing, no contact lenses.
  • Stress the need for close follow-up (daily or as recommended).
  • Communicate urgency calmly and empathetically.

Self-Assessment Rubric

Score each category 0–2 (0 = not addressed, 1 = partial, 2 = complete).

AI-style Feedback (beta)

Example 10/10 response:
“What’s going on today is a bacterial infection of the clear front window of your eye, called bacterial keratitis. The germs have gotten into the surface of the cornea and are causing the pain, redness, light sensitivity, and blurry vision you’re feeling.

This is something we take very seriously because if it is not treated quickly and aggressively, it can leave a permanent scar on the cornea and, in severe cases, can even threaten the eye itself. The good news is that you came in promptly, and we can start treatment right away.

The most important part of treatment is strong antibiotic eye drops that you’ll use very frequently at first – often every hour while you’re awake. We’ll give you very specific instructions on how often to use them. It’s also essential that you do not wear your contact lenses at all while this is healing, and that you throw away the current lenses and case so we’re not re-exposing your eye to the same germs.

This type of infection is strongly linked to sleeping in lenses and exposing them to tap water, so in the future we’ll talk about much stricter hygiene and safer wearing habits if you go back into contacts. For now, our focus is on clearing the infection and protecting your vision.

I’m going to have you back for very close follow-up, usually tomorrow and then again after that, so we can make sure the infection is responding. If at any point you notice your pain suddenly getting worse, your vision dropping rapidly, or the eye becoming much more red or swollen, those are signs to be seen urgently – even after hours. Does this explanation make sense, and what questions do you have before we get started with the drops?”
