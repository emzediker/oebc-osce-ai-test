OEBC Mock OSCE – Station 57

Other • Ethics & Record-Keeping (Interactive)

Step 1 – Read the Station

Scenario:

A 52-year-old patient presents for a routine eye exam and vision check for driver’s licence renewal. With best correction, they see only 20/50 in the better-seeing eye, which does not meet the legal standard in your jurisdiction.

When informed, the patient becomes upset and says: “I’ve been driving for 30 years and never had an accident. If I lose my licence, I’ll lose my job. Can’t you just write that I passed? No one will know.”

Your task: Explain to the patient:

  • Why you cannot falsify records or licensing forms
  • Your legal and professional obligations
  • What options or next steps you can provide
  • How you will document the conversation

Step 2 – Speak (Type) Your Response

Key Communication & Ethics Points (What OEBC Expects)

  • Start with empathy for the patient's fear of losing independence/employment.
  • Clear boundary: Records and licensing forms must be accurate.
  • Legal/professional obligations: Safety, regulatory standards, and documentation rules.
  • Offer options: Possible treatment, referral, restricted licence options, re-testing.
  • Professional tone: Calm, respectful, supportive.
  • Documentation: State what will be entered into the record.

Self-Assessment Rubric

Score each domain 0–2 (Total possible = 10).

Domain Criteria
Empathy & Rapport Acknowledged the patient’s concerns and frustration.
Ethical Boundary Stated clearly that falsifying documents is unacceptable.
Legal / Professional Duty Explained safety obligations and regulatory requirements.
Management & Options Provided appropriate alternatives and supports.
Professionalism & Clarity Clear, respectful, organized communication.

Model 10/10 Response (What a High-Scoring Answer Sounds Like)

“I completely understand how stressful this feels. Losing a licence can affect your independence and your job, so I really hear your concern. My job, though, is to document exactly what I find today. I’m not allowed to change the numbers or say you met the standard when you didn’t — not only is it unethical, but it’s also part of the legal requirements for keeping people safe on the road, including you.

What I can do is talk through options. Sometimes vision can improve with new glasses, or there may be treatable eye conditions like cataracts that a surgeon can address. We can refer you today if needed. Depending on our provincial rules, there may also be the possibility of a restricted licence, or re-testing after treatment.

I also want you to know that I’ll document our conversation clearly in your chart so there’s a record of what we discussed, and I’ll support you through the next steps. You’re not alone in this — we just have to follow the correct legal process to make sure the plan is safe and appropriate.”
